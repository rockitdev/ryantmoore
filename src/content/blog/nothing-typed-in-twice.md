> If someone on your team retypes the same information into a second tool, that's not a task. That's a leak.

Most businesses I talk to don't have a software problem. They have plenty of software: a booking tool, an accounting tool, a calendar, a CRM, a phone system. Each one works fine on its own.

The problem is the space *between* them.

## What the leak looks like

It rarely looks dramatic. It looks like this:

- A job gets booked in one system, then someone copies it into the calendar by hand.
- An invoice gets written in the field, then typed into the books that evening.
- A customer's new phone number gets updated in one place, and stays wrong in three others.

Each copy takes a minute or two. Nobody complains, because it's always been done that way. But minutes turn into hours, and the retyping is the *good* outcome. The bad outcome is the copy that doesn't happen: the booking that never reaches the calendar, the invoice that never reaches the books, the follow-up that never goes out. Those aren't minutes. Those are missed jobs and unpaid work.

## Why it happens

Software companies build tools, not connections. Your booking tool's job is bookings. Your accounting tool's job is the books. Neither company gets paid to make sure the two agree with each other, so out of the box they usually don't.

That gap is left for someone in your business to fill. Usually with copy-and-paste.

## What "connected" actually means

When two tools are properly connected, information moves on its own, the moment it exists:

- A booking lands, and it's already in your calendar.
- A job is marked done, and the invoice is already in your books.
- A customer updates their details once, and every system agrees.

No one does anything. That's the point. The connection isn't a feature you use. It's a leak that stopped existing.

## Where to start

You don't need to connect everything at once. Start with one question: **what does someone in your business type in twice?**

Ask the person who does the admin, not the person who bought the software. They'll answer immediately, because it's the most annoying part of their day. That answer is almost always the highest-value connection you can build, and it's usually a much smaller project than people expect.
